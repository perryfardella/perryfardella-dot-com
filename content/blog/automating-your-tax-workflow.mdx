Tax season is the same story every year: hundreds of client files, a dozen spreadsheets, and a calendar that fills up faster than you can clear it. After fifteen years as a CPA and the last several building software on the side, I've come to believe the bottleneck is rarely about knowledge — it's about process.

The biggest wins I've found aren't from replacing accountants with software. They're from eliminating the low-value data-wrangling that accountants shouldn't be doing in the first place.

## Where the time actually goes

Roughly 40% of the time I used to spend on individual tax returns was moving data between systems — bank exports into Excel, Excel into the tax software, the tax software back into a client summary. Each transfer introduced the possibility of error and none of it required any accounting judgement.

The fix was building a thin integration layer: a small script that pulled reconciled transactions from Xero, applied our standard categorisation rules, and dropped a formatted summary directly into our return template. The first version took a weekend. It saved several hours per client per year.

## What "automation" actually means in practice

For most practices, automation doesn't mean AI or complex pipelines. It means:

1. **Standardising your inputs.** If every client delivers data in the same format, you can process them the same way.
2. **Templating your outputs.** Every letter, every summary, every checklist should start from a template, not a blank page.
3. **Connecting the gaps.** The manual steps between systems are where time disappears. Even a simple export/import script run once a week compounds into meaningful hours over a year.

## The tax agent's unfair advantage

As accountants, we have something developers often lack: we know exactly what the output needs to look like and why. That domain knowledge is the hardest part of building accounting software. The technical execution — writing a script, setting up a scheduled job — is learnable.

If you're a CPA curious about automation, you don't need to become a software engineer. You need just enough technical literacy to describe the problem precisely and either build a simple version yourself or work effectively with someone who can.

That intersection is where the most interesting work happens.
